Persona 1 — The Unaware School Girl
Profile: 14 years old, Class 9, small town Maharashtra, government school
Research source: A study of 460 girls aged 15–18 in a residential college in Andhra Pradesh found 9.13% met PCOS criteria — most of them undiagnosed. Google Play
Reality: Has irregular periods but assumes it's normal puberty. Nobody at school talks about this. Her mother had the same symptoms and was never diagnosed either. She doesn't know the word PCOS exists.
Key pain: Zero awareness. No one in her environment — family, school, doctor — has connected her symptoms to a condition.
What she needs from CycleWise: The screening form. Just the screening form. That one interaction could change the trajectory of her health.

Persona 2 — The College Student Managing Alone
Profile: 19 years old, CS student, metro city, diagnosed at 14
Research source: Indian women with PCOS report poor quality of life specifically in weight and emotion domains, with a one-year average delay in seeking help and seven months of diagnostic delay after that. Wikipedia
Reality: This is Archana — our real volunteer. Knows her condition deeply. Struggles emotionally. Feels like a bad person because of behaviour she can't always control. Has gone to AI for emotional support because humans didn't understand.
Key pain: Emotional isolation. Nobody around her understands that her mood swings have a hormonal basis, not a character flaw.
What she needs from CycleWise: Saathi. The community. Personal insights that validate her experience with data.

Persona 3 — The Newly Diagnosed Girl in Shock
Profile: 20 years old, first year college, Mumbai, diagnosed 3 weeks ago
Research source: Qualitative interviews with newly diagnosed PCOS patients revealed three dominant emotional themes — shock at diagnosis, anxiety about childbearing, and confusion about where to get reliable information. Google Play
Reality: Just came back from the gynecologist. Was told she has PCOS, given a prescription, and sent home in 10 minutes. Has spent the last 3 days googling everything and scaring herself. Doesn't know what's true and what's not.
Key pain: Information overload combined with emotional shock. Too much contradictory information online and no trusted guide.
What she needs from CycleWise: A calm, clear onboarding that explains what PCOS actually means for daily life. Not clinical, not scary. Just honest and structured.

Persona 4 — The Rural Girl With No Access
Profile: 17 years old, Madhya Pradesh, village, no smartphone, uses a shared family phone
Research source: A qualitative study in Madhya Pradesh found significant barriers to PCOS diagnosis in rural India including low awareness, cultural stigma around menstruation, limited healthcare access, and heavy reliance on traditional treatments. Instagram
Reality: Has severe irregular periods and significant facial hair growth. Family attributes it to stress or poor diet. Nearest gynaecologist is 40km away. If she ever reached the screening form via a school visit, that PDF report would be the only medical guidance she'd ever receive.
Key pain: Access. Geography. Stigma around even discussing periods at home.
What she needs from CycleWise: The screening form output to be useful even without downloading the app. The parent PDF needs to work for families with low health literacy.

Persona 5 — The Working Woman Under Pressure
Profile: 26 years old, software engineer, Bengaluru, PCOS 6 years, on medication
Research source: An ethnographic study of professional urban Indian women with PCOS in Mumbai found that work stress directly disrupts hormonal balance and that women are constantly negotiating PCOS management around demanding professional lives. Hobo.Video
Reality: Managing well most of the time but work sprint weeks destroy her cycle. Has been to 4 different doctors. Each gives slightly different advice. Has never had a single doctor visit where she felt fully understood.
Key pain: No tool helps her communicate her full history to a new doctor quickly. Starts from scratch every appointment.
What she needs from CycleWise: Doctor prep report. Symptom history. Monthly pattern that shows work stress correlating to flare-ups.

Persona 6 — The Girl With Body Image Damage
Profile: 20 years old, college student, Mumbai, developed an eating disorder after PCOS diagnosis
Research source: Sarah Patil, a 20-year-old student in Mumbai, developed an eating disorder after being diagnosed with PCOS — reaching a point of self-harm. Studies confirm women with irregular cycles have significantly lower self-esteem and higher body dissatisfaction. Daswritingservices
Reality: Was told by her doctor to lose weight to manage PCOS. Started restricting food. Cycle got worse. Now in a harmful loop of restriction and guilt. Doesn't trust health apps because they all show calorie counters and BMI charts that trigger her.
Key pain: Existing health apps make her condition worse. She needs body-neutral, shame-free language throughout.
What she needs from CycleWise: An app that never mentions weight loss, BMI, or calories unless she specifically asks. Symptom tracking and emotional support only. This is why CycleWise must be built carefully — the wrong language causes real harm.

Persona 7 — The Exhausted College Student
Profile: 20 years old, Kolkata, managing college pressure and PCOS simultaneously
Research source: Nayanika Mukherjee, a 20-year-old student from Kolkata, reports extreme lethargy and fatigue accompanying PCOS because of the strenuous demands of college life — and doctors attribute the increased prevalence to academic and lifestyle pressure on younger generations. Daswritingservices
Reality: Misses classes because of fatigue. Friends assume she is lazy. She knows she has PCOS but has no idea that her exhaustion is a symptom, not a personality trait. Feels ashamed and behind her peers.
Key pain: Fatigue is invisible. Nobody takes it seriously. She needs to be able to show people — including herself — that her exhaustion has a real cause.
What she needs from CycleWise: Daily check-in that tracks energy levels over time. Personal insight that shows "your energy dips correlate with the luteal phase every month." That data validates her experience.

Persona 8 — The Woman Who Was Dismissed by Doctors
Profile: 24 years old, Delhi, spent 3 years trying to get a diagnosis
Research source: A mixed-method study at PGIMER Chandigarh found that nearly 45% of PCOS patients had no information about the condition, only 9.1% received information from their doctors, and delayed and unsuccessful treatment was described as "very stressful" by patients themselves. PubMed Central
Reality: Went to three different doctors before getting diagnosed. The first said her periods being irregular was normal. The second gave her contraceptive pills without explaining why. The third finally diagnosed her. She is now deeply distrustful of the medical system.
Key pain: Medical gaslighting over years. She knows her body but no one believed her.
What she needs from CycleWise: A tool that takes her self-reported symptoms seriously and gives her language and data to advocate for herself in medical settings.

Persona 9 — The Married Woman Worried About Fertility
Profile: 28 years old, Pune, married 1 year, PCOS, family pressure about children beginning
Research source: Qualitative research found that anxiety about childbearing was one of the three dominant emotional themes in PCOS patients — present from diagnosis onwards and intensifying significantly with age and social pressure. Google Play
Reality: Has been managing PCOS for 6 years. Was fine until marriage. Now faces constant questions from family about when she will have children. Her PCOS makes this deeply anxiety-inducing. Has no space to talk about this honestly — even her husband doesn't fully understand.
Key pain: Social and family pressure colliding with a health condition she can't fully control. The emotional weight is enormous and she has no community that truly understands this specific situation.
What she needs from CycleWise: The community most of all. Specifically a space with other married women managing PCOS and fertility anxiety together — people who understand the exact cultural pressure she is under.

Persona 10 — The Mother of a Newly Diagnosed Teenager
Profile: 42 years old, Chennai, her 16-year-old daughter just received a high-risk screening result
Research source: Specialists emphasise that early diagnosis and lifestyle modification in teenagers can prevent long-term consequences including Type 2 diabetes, obesity, insulin resistance, and infertility — making parent involvement critical. Google Play
Reality: She herself had irregular periods her whole life but was never diagnosed. Now her daughter has a high risk screening result and she doesn't know what to do with it. Is it serious? What does it mean? What should she say to her daughter? What does she ask the doctor?
Key pain: She wants to support her daughter but has no information and no guide. The medical system is confusing and she doesn't want to panic her daughter unnecessarily.
What she needs from CycleWise: The parent PDF from the screening tool — written in plain language, respectful, non-alarmist. Tells her exactly what the next step is and what questions to ask the doctor.
